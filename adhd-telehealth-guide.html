Assessment consent — say at start
"Before we begin, I want to be upfront. Today is a holistic assessment — I'm looking at everything, not just ADHD. I can't guarantee a diagnosis because that depends on what I find. And even if I do diagnose ADHD, I can't guarantee we'll end up on stimulant medication — that depends on your medical history and what's clinically safe. What I can guarantee is I want to do the very best by you."
Financial consent — cover before starting
"I also want to be transparent about cost. Today is one appointment, but depending on what comes up there may be follow-up — for example to monitor how medication is going during titration. I'll only recommend follow-up when it's genuinely clinically necessary and I'll be as efficient with your time and money as I safely can."

▶Start open — don't lead

2Inattention — Criterion AAdults ≥17: ≥5 symptoms · Under-17: ≥6 · Present ≥6 months · Inconsistent with developmental level
1Careless mistakes / fails close attention to detail
"Do you often make careless mistakes at work, study, or in daily tasks?" "Do you miss details in reading, forms, or emails — even important ones?" "Rush through things and then notice obvious errors afterward?"
Look for: Recurring pattern across settings, not one-off. Not solely explained by rushing due to anxiety.
2Difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play
"Can you only concentrate when something is genuinely interesting or urgent?" "Do you drift off mid-conversation, meeting, or lecture — even when you want to focus?" "Do you re-read sentences over and over without taking anything in?"
Look for: Present even for self-chosen tasks; not just boring or aversive tasks. Hyperfocus on high-interest tasks doesn't exclude ADHD.
3Doesn't seem to listen when spoken to directly
"Do people say you weren't listening even when you were right there in front of them?" "Find yourself mid-conversation thinking about something completely different?" "Have to ask people to repeat what they just said?"
Look for: Happens even in important conversations, not just disinterest. "Mind goes elsewhere" rather than deliberate tuning out.
4Doesn't follow through / fails to finish tasks
"Lots of half-finished projects or tasks — at home, work, study?" "Start things with good intentions but drift away before finishing?" "Not because you lost interest in the goal — just somehow stop partway through?"
Look for: Executive dropout, not oppositionality or loss of motivation. Task initiation often fine; completion the problem.
5Difficulty organising tasks and activities
"How would you describe your ability to organise your time, tasks, and belongings?" "Hard to break a big project into steps and work through it systematically?" "Workspace, digital files, or finances chronically disorganised despite effort?"
Look for: Not just "messy" — actual functional impairment. Time blindness (underestimates how long things take) is a core feature here.
6Avoids tasks requiring sustained mental effort
"Put off paperwork, admin, forms, or writing — even when the stakes are high?" "Does starting certain tasks feel almost physically aversive or impossible?" "Do you come alive once in crisis or deadline pressure but can't start without it?"
Look for: Avoidance driven by executive difficulty (task initiation), not anxiety or defiance. "I know I need to, I want to, I just can't start."
7Loses things necessary for tasks (keys, phone, wallet, documents)
"Frequently lose keys, phone, wallet, charger, or other everyday items?" "How much time each week searching for things you've put down 'somewhere'?" "Put objects in 'safe' places and then can't find them?"
Look for: Chronic, effortful, frustrating pattern — not occasional forgetfulness. Automated/habitual storage absent.
8Easily distracted by extraneous stimuli or unrelated thoughts
"Does nearby noise or movement easily break your concentration?" "Does your mind jump to unrelated thoughts unprompted when you're trying to focus?" "Is open-plan or background-noise environments especially hard to work in?"
Look for: Both external (noise/movement) AND internal (unrelated thoughts) distractibility count. Internal is more prominent in adults.
9Forgetful in daily activities (chores, appointments, returning calls)
"Forget appointments, tasks, or things someone just told you?" "Walk into a room and forget why, or lose track of what you were doing mid-task?" "Rely heavily on phone alarms, notes, or lists just to function day-to-day?"
Look for: Working memory (hold + manipulate info) AND prospective memory (remember to do things) failures. Coping systems shouldn't mask severity.
3Hyperactivity / Impulsivity — Criterion AAdults ≥17: ≥5 symptoms · In adults H/I often = internal restlessness, not overtObserve during the interview
1Fidgets / taps / squirms when seated
"Find yourself fidgeting, tapping, or physically restless when sitting still?" "Hard to sit through long meetings, lectures, or meals without moving?" "Notice others react to movement or noise you're making?"
Observe now: leg jiggling, pen tapping, chair swivelling, posture shifting. Adults often redirect motor restlessness into smaller movements.
2Often leaves seat when staying seated is expected
"Do you find yourself getting up frequently when you're supposed to stay seated?" "In meetings, classes, or restaurants — do you find reasons to get up and move?"
In adults: often expressed as inability to stay seated for full duration; finds excuses to leave, stands to work, paces. Less common but present.
3Runs about / climbs — in adults: inner sense of restlessness
"Do you have a constant inner sense of restlessness — like an internal motor that won't stop?" "Do you feel much better when you're moving or doing something rather than sitting still?" "Is it hard to just 'be' without needing to keep busy?"
Key adult presentation: internal not external. "I look calm on the outside but inside I'm buzzing." This is the most clinically important H/I item in adults.
4Unable to engage in leisure activities quietly
"Hard to genuinely relax or do quiet, passive activities?" "Always need to be doing something — multiple tabs, TV on in the background, phone in hand?" "Feel uncomfortable or irritable when forced to slow down or be idle?"
Look for: Chronic restlessness during downtime; always needs stimulation. Not just a preference for activity — feels driven.
5"On the go" / driven by a motor
"Do people say you always seem to be 'on the go' or that they can't keep up with you?" "Do you feel driven to keep busy even when you'd prefer to slow down?" "When you do stop, does your mind immediately race to the next thing?"
Look for: Consistent across settings; internal experience matches external observation. Not driven by anxiety or avoidance of stillness (overlap — clarify).
6Talks excessively
"Do people say you talk too much, too fast, or dominate conversations?" "Do you go off on tangents and struggle to get back to the point?" "Still talking when you've already made your point?"
Look for: Pattern across relationships, not just with specific people. Causes social friction. Note: tangential speech is observable in the interview.
7Blurts out answers / finishes others' sentences
"Do you blurt out answers before questions are fully finished?" "Find yourself finishing other people's sentences?" "Jump in with a response before fully hearing what was asked?"
Observable now: does the patient interrupt, pre-empt, or finish your questions? Noting this in the MSE adds direct clinical evidence.
8Difficulty waiting their turn / impulsive decisions
"Find queuing, waiting on hold, or waiting your turn very difficult?" "Make impulsive decisions — purchases, messages, commitments — and regret them later?" "Hard to wait in conversation — feel you must respond immediately?"
Look for: Impulsivity across domains (social, financial, occupational). Acts before thinking; regret is common. Distinct from deliberate risk-taking.
9Interrupts or intrudes on others
"Do you often interrupt conversations or activities even when you're trying not to?" "Do you jump into others' conversations or tasks without being invited?" "Do you take over tasks from others because you can't wait for them to finish?"
Look for: Pattern across multiple relationships and settings. Not just with specific people or in high-conflict situations.
4Developmental History — Criteria B · C · DB = pre-age 12 onset · C = ≥2 settings · D = functional impairment
BPrimary school / early childhood pattern
"When you picture yourself in primary school — what sort of kid were you at school and at home?" "What did teachers or parents keep correcting you for, over and over?" "'Bright but not applying yourself'? 'Could do better'? Daydreamy, chatty, fidgety, always on the go?" "Did things seem inconsistent — you could do it sometimes, but not reliably?" "What themes would I expect to see repeated across your primary school reports?"
Criterion B: symptoms must have been present before age 12, but don't need to have been formally identified. Retrospective recognition is common and valid.
BAdolescence — what happened when structure dropped?
"What happened when school became less supervised and more self-directed?" "Did homework, deadlines, studying, or organisation become much harder in high school?" "Did the presentation shift from obvious hyperactivity to internal restlessness, procrastination, disorganisation?" "Did risk-taking, emotional impulsivity, or substance use start showing up?" "Did teachers or family feel you had potential you weren't living up to?"
Many late-diagnosed adults managed primary school but decompensated when external structure fell away in adolescence. This is a key diagnostic window.
ACurrent adult pattern — what fails despite insight and effort?
"How do these traits show up now — in work, study, home admin, finances, or relationships?" "What happens for you with tasks that are boring, repetitive, delayed-reward, or paperwork-heavy?" "Do you function much better only when there's urgency, novelty, pressure, or strong personal interest?" "What failures of everyday executive function keep recurring despite your insight and effort?"
The "interest-based nervous system" is characteristic: performance is variable, not consistently impaired. Look for the interest/urgency/novelty dependency.
+Masking and compensation — adjusts true severity estimate
"Was your functioning maintained because someone was externally organising you — parent, teacher, partner, workplace?" "Did intelligence, perfectionism, anxiety, or overwork help you compensate and hide the gaps?" "Did things fall apart when those supports dropped away?" "Were you functioning at the cost of exhaustion, chronic stress, or shame?"
Masking is common in women, high-IQ individuals, and those raised in structured environments. The real severity is what emerges when scaffolding is removed.
CCross-situational — symptoms in ≥2 settings
"Has this pattern shown up across more than one setting — school, home, work, social life?" "Were there settings where problems completely disappeared, and if so, what was different there?" "Did people in different parts of your life notice similar traits independently?" "Was the problem limited to one teacher, one job, one relationship — or has it broadly recurred?"
Criterion C threshold: ≥2 settings. Setting-specific problems suggest situational factors. If it truly disappears in all unstructured environments, reconsider. Collateral evidence helps.
DFunctional impairment — what did it cost?
"What did these traits cost you academically, socially, emotionally, financially, or occupationally?" "What opportunities did you miss because of inconsistency or poor follow-through?" "Were there repeated consequences — underachievement, warnings, unfinished courses, job instability, relationship strain?" "Did the impairment happen even when you were genuinely trying hard?"
Criterion D: More than mild interference; clear negative impact. Distress alone isn't sufficient — there must be functional consequences. "Coping" with enormous effort still counts.
5Differential Diagnosis — Screen AllThese are also common comorbidities — not either/or · tap ? for distinguishing questions
Depression
Mood-contingent vs always present? Episodic vs lifelong pattern?
  • "When your mood is normal, do attention and organisation problems still remain?"
  • "Did concentration problems begin only during depressive episodes?"
  • "Has low mood been present more days than not for 2+ years, or in discrete episodes?"
  • "Does the picture feel more like low mood and drive, or lifelong distractibility?"
Bipolar-spectrum
Discrete elevated/irritable episodes with reduced sleep? Energy cycling?
  • "Have there been clear episodes of elevated or irritable mood with reduced need for sleep?"
  • "Do periods of high energy and risk-taking alternate with more normal or low periods?"
  • "Are there recurrent swings between low-grade depression and elevated energy?"
  • "Is distractibility tied mainly to mood episodes rather than being lifelong?"
Anxiety disorder
Mind stuck on worry vs generally distractible? Anxiety-contingent vs constant?
  • "Does your mind wander due to general distractibility, or because it gets stuck on worry?"
  • "When anxiety settles, do focus and organisation improve substantially?"
  • "Is avoidance driven more by fear or perfectionism than boredom or difficulty starting?"
  • "Do symptoms largely resolve in familiar or low-stakes settings?"
PTSD
Onset after trauma? Trigger-contingent vs constant? Hypervigilance vs distractibility?
  • "Did concentration problems begin after trauma exposure?"
  • "Is attention hijacked by hypervigilance, intrusive memories, or dissociation?"
  • "Do symptoms spike around triggers rather than being constant?"
  • "Is there a history of prolonged or repeated interpersonal trauma?"
Autism spectrum disorder
Social communication differences? Rigidity/sensory overload vs distractibility?
  • "Have there been longstanding differences in social communication and flexibility?"
  • "Are task problems driven more by rigidity or sensory overload than distractibility?"
  • "Were autistic traits present before academic attention issues were noticed?"
OCD-spectrum
Time lost to rituals or intrusive thoughts? Compulsion-driven lateness?
  • "Is time lost to rituals, checking, or intrusive thoughts rather than distractibility?"
  • "Are you late because of compulsions rather than losing track of time?"
  • "When compulsions are managed, do executive problems substantially resolve?"
Personality disorder
Identity/relationship instability predominates? Affect-contingent impulsivity?
  • "Is the main difficulty unstable relationships, identity, and affect rather than inattention?"
  • "Are impulsive acts mostly linked to relational crises or intense emotional states?"
  • "When affect is regulated, do executive symptoms largely resolve?"
  • "Is there chronic emptiness or identity disturbance beyond time-blindness?"
Schizophrenia-spectrum
Positive symptoms? Change in concentration tracking psychosis onset?
  • "Are there positive symptoms such as hallucinations, delusions, or disorganised thinking?"
  • "Did the concentration picture change markedly around the time of psychotic symptoms?"
  • "Has there been a discrete period of psychotic symptoms?"
Sleep disorder
Cognitive problems track sleep quality? OSA, circadian, or insomnia symptoms?
  • "Are you consistently short on sleep or unrefreshed on waking?"
  • "Do you snore loudly, stop breathing, or have restless legs at night?"
  • "Do cognitive problems track sleep quality more than anything else?"
Substance-induced
Resolves or substantially improves on sustained abstinence? Active SUD?
  • "Could stimulant, cannabis, alcohol, or other substance or medication use explain or worsen the picture?"
  • "Do cognitive symptoms resolve or substantially improve during sustained abstinence?"
  • "Is there an active substance use disorder that needs prioritising?"
Neurodegenerative disorder
Decline from higher baseline? Progressive vs lifelong pattern?
  • "Is there a noticeable decline from a previously higher level of functioning?"
  • "Are memory, language, or processing speed declining over months to years?"
  • "Is this a change from baseline rather than a lifelong pattern?"
Acquired brain injury
Post-injury onset or significant worsening? Formal neuropsych assessment done?
  • "Did cognitive difficulties begin or worsen significantly after a head injury?"
  • "Has there been formal neuropsychological assessment post-injury?"
Binge eating disorder
Impulsivity mainly expressed through eating? Improves independent of eating behaviour?
  • "Are there recurrent episodes of eating large amounts with loss of control and marked distress?"
  • "Is impulsivity mainly expressed through eating rather than across domains?"
  • "Does the attention picture improve independently of eating behaviour?"
Medical / neurological cause
Post-illness onset? Thyroid, anaemia, seizures, autoimmune, medications?
  • "Did symptoms start after a medical illness, injury, or medication change?"
  • "Could thyroid, anaemia, seizures, autoimmune, or other medical issues be contributing?"
  • "Has relevant medical investigation been completed?"
Suicidal ideation
Active ideation? Plan or intent? Protective factors?
  • "Are there current thoughts of suicide or self-harm?"
  • "Has there been any recent planning, intent, or preparatory behaviour?"
  • "What protective factors are in place and how robust are they?"

!Safety Screen — Run Before Prescribing
Cardiac Safety — 10 Items to Screen (tap ? for clinical note)
Absolute contraindication Current MAOI use. Do not prescribe stimulants or atomoxetine until full washout.
Exertional chest pain / unexplained SOB / exercise intolerance
Possible structural heart disease or ischaemia. Defer stimulants and obtain medical or cardiology assessment first.
Palpitations / syncope or fainting
Possible arrhythmia or conduction disorder. Obtain history, exam and ECG, and usually defer stimulants until clarified.
Dizziness on standing
Check orthostatic vitals and hydration first. Use extra caution with clonidine or guanfacine as they can worsen hypotension.
Family history: sudden cardiac death <40 / unexplained syncope or seizures
Suggests possible inherited arrhythmia. Get ECG and have a low threshold for cardiology review before stimulants.
Family history: cardiomyopathy / long QT / channelopathy
Suggests inherited structural or electrical disease. Seek cardiology input before stimulant treatment.
Family history: early-onset coronary artery disease (M <55, F <65)
Increases vascular risk relevance in adults. Optimise risk factors and monitor blood pressure carefully when using stimulants.
Known congenital or structural heart disease
Liaise with the treating cardiologist before prescribing stimulants. If used, start low and monitor BP, pulse and symptoms closely.
Known arrhythmia / tachyarrhythmia / long QT syndrome
Higher risk with sympathomimetic drugs. Use stimulants only with cardiology oversight and consider non-stimulants where appropriate.
Hypertension
Control blood pressure before starting stimulants and monitor regularly during treatment.
Current MAOI use — ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION
Do not prescribe stimulants or atomoxetine until an appropriate washout period is completed. This is an absolute contraindication.
Medical Safety — 18 Items (tap ? for prescribing note)
Epilepsy or seizure disorder
Stimulants may lower seizure threshold. Check recent control and neurology input, start low and monitor.
Glaucoma
Avoid stimulants and atomoxetine in narrow-angle glaucoma. Consider non-stimulant alternatives.
Thyroid disorder
Treat or stabilise hyperthyroidism before starting stimulants due to additive sympathomimetic effects.
Raynaud's phenomenon
Stimulants can worsen vasospasm. Monitor digits and consider dose reduction or switch if symptomatic.
Tic disorder
Stimulants can exacerbate tics in some patients. Consider atomoxetine or alpha-2 agonists, or cautious stimulant trial with close monitoring.
Hepatic impairment
Atomoxetine exposure increases significantly in moderate-severe hepatic impairment. Use lower doses and monitor LFTs.
Renal impairment
Clonidine and guanfacine may accumulate in renal impairment. Titrate slowly and monitor for hypotension and sedation.
Pregnancy
Use non-pharmacological strategies where possible. If prescribing in pregnancy, use the lowest effective dose and agents with the best safety data, with obstetric input.
Breastfeeding
Stimulants pass into breast milk in small amounts. Methylphenidate has the lowest excretion. Discuss risks/benefits with the patient and monitor infant for irritability or feeding changes.
Coronary artery disease
Stimulants increase HR and BP. Optimise vascular risk factors, consider cardiology input, start low and monitor closely.
Obesity with metabolic syndrome
Stimulants may reduce appetite and weight. Monitor weight, blood pressure and metabolic parameters during treatment.
Low BMI
Appetite suppression can be problematic. Prefer non-stimulants or very cautious stimulant use with close weight monitoring.
Current or recent eating disorder
Stimulants may worsen dietary restriction. Avoid or minimise stimulants and prioritise nutritional rehabilitation.
Obstructive sleep apnoea
Optimise sleep and OSA treatment first. Time doses to minimise insomnia. Consider alpha-2 agonists for nocturnal hyperarousal.
Benign prostatic hyperplasia
Atomoxetine can worsen urinary retention. Monitor lower urinary tract symptoms or avoid in severe cases.
End-stage renal disease
Use clonidine and guanfacine very cautiously. Coordinate with nephrology and monitor haemodynamics carefully.
Chronic dizziness or high falls risk
Alpha-2 agonists can increase orthostatic symptoms and falls risk. Minimise dose and monitor standing blood pressure.
Endocrine/autonomic disorder with labile BP (e.g. Addison disease, autonomic neuropathy)
Both stimulants (raise BP) and alpha-2 agonists (lower BP, with rebound on cessation) can destabilise blood pressure. Titrate very cautiously with frequent postural checks.

4Side Effects & Monitoring
Side Effect Reference — Stimulants
Common (>10%)
  • Decreased appetite (usually most prominent mid-morning to afternoon)
  • Weight loss (dose-dependent; significant in children)
  • Insomnia / sleep onset delay (timing-dependent — dose too late)
  • Headache (usually resolves in first 1–2 weeks)
  • Dry mouth

1Understanding ADHD
Explaining ADHD

Key message: ADHD is not a deficit of attention — it is a regulation problem. The brain runs on interest, urgency, and novelty rather than importance alone. When something genuinely captures your interest, you can hyperfocus brilliantly. When something feels dull or unrewarding, starting it can feel genuinely impossible — not because of laziness, but because the brain's motivational circuitry works differently.

Neurobiology: The prefrontal cortex — the region managing planning, organising, task initiation, working memory, and emotional regulation — is underactive and less connected to the rest of the brain in ADHD. This reduces dopamine and noradrenaline signalling, which are the neurotransmitters responsible for focus, motivation, and impulse control. Medication restores these neurotransmitters in the prefrontal cortex.

What this looks like daily: Starting tasks feels insurmountable. Losing the thread mid-task. Forgetting why you walked into a room. Emotions that hit fast and hard. Acting before thinking. Constant inner restlessness. Time feeling abstract until a deadline is imminent.

Key facts: Affects 5–10% of adults. One of the most heritable conditions in medicine. ADHD medications are among the most effective treatments in all of psychiatry. When medication works well, people often describe it as finally having access to the version of themselves they always knew was there.

How ADHD Manifests

Core symptoms: Inattention, executive dysfunction (organising, starting, time management, working memory), impulsivity, and hyperactivity — often internalised in adults as a constant inner restlessness rather than visible movement.

Often missed features — the most disabling aspects are frequently not on the official list:

  • Emotional dysregulation: Emotions arrive faster and more intensely because the prefrontal "braking system" is less effective. Frustration, excitement, rejection, and disappointment hit harder. This is neurological, not a personality trait.
  • Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD): Intense emotional pain triggered by perceived rejection, criticism, or failure — even when mild or imagined. Can dominate relationships and cause avoidance of challenge. Extremely common, rarely discussed.
  • Time blindness: The internal sense of time is impaired. Time exists in two states: "now" and "not now." Deadlines days away feel unreal, producing chronic lateness and last-minute rushes.
  • Working memory deficits: The mental notepad used to hold information while doing something else is impaired — causing thought loss mid-sentence, forgetting what you walked into a room for, re-reading the same paragraph repeatedly.
  • Hyperfocus: The same dysregulation causing inattention can flip to tunnel-vision absorption where hours pass unnoticed. Same system, different direction.
  • Sleep problems, appetite dysregulation, low frustration tolerance, chronic underachievement despite high ability.

Adult presentation: Many people — especially those who are intellectually capable — compensated through school via intelligence or anxiety-driven perfectionism, then found that when demands exceeded coping strategies, everything unravelled.

How Treatable ADHD Is

Response rates: 70–80% in children, 60–75% in adults. If one stimulant class does not work, switching to the other class helps many non-responders.

The evidence base:

  • 21% reduction in all-cause mortality with ADHD medication
  • 25% reduction in unnatural-cause deaths (accidents, suicide)
  • ~13% reduction in substance use disorder risk per year on stimulants
  • 72% of measured long-term outcomes improved — academic, work, social, driving
  • Lower motor vehicle crash rates while on medication
  • Reduced criminal offending during treatment periods
  • Better employment outcomes and improved relationship stability

Key message: The risks of not treating ADHD are substantially greater than the risks of properly managed treatment.

What to Expect from Treatment

When medication is working well, most people describe:

  • Calm clarity — genuinely quiet in your own head
  • Boring-but-important tasks becoming manageable
  • More pause between feeling and reacting
  • Anxiety often improves (much ADHD anxiety is downstream from chronic underperformance)
  • Low mood lifts when you are no longer falling short of your own capabilities

What it should NOT feel like: Robotic, flat, zombie-like, or like a different person. If it feels that way, the dose is probably too high — that is a signal to adjust, not tolerate.

Starting out: We start at a low dose and increase gradually. Stimulants work the same day at therapeutic dose. Appetite suppression is common especially at lunch. The first few doses may feel unremarkable as the body acclimatises.

Missing a dose: Take it when you remember, unless it is late afternoon — then skip it. Never double up the next day.

If it stops working as well: Sleep, stress, hormones, appetite, and routine all affect medication response. Sometimes dose or timing needs review — contact your psychiatrist rather than adjusting independently.

Commonly Asked Questions

Will it change my personality? No — at therapeutic doses you feel more like yourself. Flat or robotic = dose too high.

Will I need it forever? ADHD is typically lifelong. Most prefer continuing because symptoms return when stopped. Long-term stimulant use is safe.

Are stimulants addictive? Used correctly for ADHD, they actually lower addiction risk. Careful prescribing is why robust diagnosis matters.

Can I drive? Yes. No restrictions in Australia. Evidence shows lower crash rates on medication.

Coffee on stimulants? Moderate is fine (1–2 cups morning). Excess worsens anxiety and insomnia.

What if it does not work? Try the other stimulant class. If stimulants not tolerated, non-stimulant options are effective.

Cannabis? Avoid — worsens ADHD symptoms, reduces treatment benefit, increases psychosis risk in vulnerable individuals.

How will I know when it is working? Everyday tasks feel easier to start and finish. Your mind feels quieter, less overwhelmed. Often the change is subtle at first.

I have never taken medication before! Very common. We start low, increase gradually, review how you respond. You are not locked in.

What if it is not ADHD? Diagnosis is based on a proper assessment, not just a feeling. Doubting yourself does not mean you are wrong.

Why was I not diagnosed as a kid? Many adults were missed — especially if they were bright, quiet, anxious, or coping reasonably well.

Do I have autism too? ADHD and autism overlap in 50–70% of cases. If you have long-standing social differences, sensory sensitivities, or a strong need for routine, it is worth discussing.

Can I travel with stimulant medications? Usually yes. Keep them in original packaging with a prescription or doctor letter. Check rules for each country.

Can I let someone else try my medication? No. Never share prescription medication. Sharing controlled substances is illegal.

The meds are not working as well anymore — why? Sleep, stress, hormones, appetite, and routine all affect medication response. Sometimes dose or timing needs review.

How do I not lean into the diagnosis as an excuse? Use it as a tool, not an identity. It should help you understand yourself and make better choices, not limit you.

Key Side Effects & Monitoring

Common stimulant side effects: Reduced appetite (especially lunch), dry mouth, insomnia if taken late, increased heart rate, headache (usually resolves 2–3 weeks), irritability, jaw clenching, rebound as medication wears off.

Less common: Elevated blood pressure, emotional blunting at higher doses (reduce dose — do not tolerate), anxiety worsening in some.

Monitoring: BP and heart rate at each review. GP for routine monitoring between psychiatric appointments.

Red flags — contact doctor if: Elevated mood + reduced sleep + rapid speech (possible mania), chest pain or palpitations, severe anxiety, marked personality change.

Atomoxetine-specific: Nausea initially — take with food. Rare: liver injury (jaundice, dark urine → stop and seek help urgently).

Key interactions: MAOIs absolute contraindication. Pseudoephedrine — discuss before combining. Alcohol — stimulants mask sedating effects, risk of drinking more than intended.

Dexamphetamine IR (immediate release)

How it works: Stimulant — increases dopamine and noradrenaline by blocking recycling AND triggering extra release. Slightly stronger effect than methylphenidate, with slightly higher side effect risk.

Practical details: Short-acting tablet. Effects begin 30–45 min, last 4–6 hrs. Usually 2–3x/day. Avoid acidic drinks at same time; take after food to reduce nausea.

What to expect: Improved task initiation and sustained effort, quieter internal restlessness, better impulse control. Appetite suppression common at lunchtime. Rebound (mood/focus drop as it wears off) can occur.

Common side effects: Reduced appetite, dry mouth, insomnia if taken too late, increased heart rate, headache (settles 2–3 weeks), irritability/anxiety in some, jaw clenching, rebound.

Interactions: MAOIs — absolute contraindication. Pseudoephedrine decongestants — discuss with doctor. Acidic drinks reduce absorption.

Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine)

How it works: Dexamphetamine in prodrug form — released gradually after swallowing for a smoother, longer effect. Very low abuse potential by design (must be metabolised to become active).

Practical details: Capsule — swallow whole or open and dissolve in water. Effects start within 1–2 hrs, last 10–12 hrs (longest-acting stimulant in Australia). Once daily in the morning. Avoid acidic drinks.

What to expect: Smooth, consistent effect with fewer peaks and troughs than IR. Useful for sustained evening coverage or when rebound from dex IR is difficult. Appetite suppression common.

Common side effects: Reduced appetite (often improves after first weeks), dry mouth, insomnia if taken too late, headache, irritability as it wears off in the evening, increased heart rate.

Interactions: MAOIs — absolute contraindication. Acidic drinks reduce absorption.

Ritalin IR / Artige (methylphenidate IR)

How it works: Blocks dopamine and noradrenaline transporters (reuptake inhibition only — does NOT trigger release). Slightly milder effect than dexamphetamine.

Practical details: Short-acting tablet. Effects begin 30–45 min, last 3–4 hrs. Usually 2–3x/day. Avoid acidic drinks, vitamin C, citric acid at dosing time — reduces absorption. Take after food to reduce nausea.

Common side effects: Reduced appetite at lunchtime, headache (settles 2–3 weeks), insomnia if afternoon dose too late, increased heart rate, jitteriness or anxiety in some, stomach discomfort.

Less common: Elevated blood pressure, rebound (mood drop as it wears off — discuss if significant), mania in bipolar disorder.

Interactions: MAOIs — absolute contraindication. Acidic drinks reduce absorption.

Ritalin LA (methylphenidate long-acting)

How it works: Same mechanism as Ritalin IR but delivers 50% immediately then 50% over the next 4–8 hours via two-pulse SODAS system. Smooth onset, longer coverage.

Practical details: Capsule — swallow whole. Approximately 8 hrs from one morning dose. Avoid acidic drinks. Some add a small Ritalin IR afternoon dose for extended coverage.

Common side effects: Reduced appetite (less pronounced than IR), insomnia if taken too late, headache, increased heart rate, jitteriness or anxiety in some.

Interactions: MAOIs — absolute contraindication. Acidic drinks reduce absorption.

Concerta (methylphenidate ER)

How it works: OROS osmotic pump: 22% delivered immediately, 78% released gradually over 10–12 hrs. Creates a slowly ascending blood level. Same reuptake inhibition mechanism as Ritalin.

Practical details: Tablet — must swallow whole (do not crush or chew). Empty shell may appear in stool (normal). 10–12 hrs from one morning dose. Avoid acidic drinks.

What to expect: Gradual ascending delivery rather than an early peak. Extended duration suits people needing afternoon/evening coverage.

Common side effects: Reduced appetite, insomnia (take in morning), headache, increased heart rate, dry mouth.

Interactions: MAOIs — absolute contraindication. Acidic drinks reduce absorption.

Strattera (atomoxetine)

How it works: NOT a stimulant. Selective noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor (SNRI). Blocks the noradrenaline transporter — which in the prefrontal cortex also indirectly increases dopamine. No abuse potential; not a controlled substance.

Practical details: Takes 4–12 weeks for full effect. Once or twice daily, titrated slowly. 24-hr coverage at therapeutic dose. Good option for anxiety, tic disorders, substance use history, or as add-on to stimulants.

Common side effects: Nausea/stomach discomfort (take with food — helps significantly), dry mouth, constipation, dizziness, mood changes during titration, urinary hesitancy, reduced appetite (milder than stimulants).

Rare but important: Liver injury — if jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), dark urine, or persistent abdominal pain, stop and seek urgent help immediately.

Interactions: Fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion dramatically increase atomoxetine levels — avoid combination. Salbutamol (Ventolin) may increase cardiovascular effects.

Intuniv (guanfacine XR)

How it works: Non-stimulant. Acts on alpha-2A adrenergic receptors in the prefrontal cortex, strengthening neural connections that control attention and impulse regulation. No abuse potential.

Practical details: Once daily, same time each day. Therapeutic effect develops over 4–8 weeks. Avoid high-fat meals at dose time. Do not stop suddenly — can cause rebound hypertension and tachycardia. Always taper under medical guidance.

What to expect: Particularly helpful for emotional dysregulation, impulsivity, and hyperactivity. Often combined with stimulants. Initial sedation usually settles.

Common side effects: Drowsiness/sedation (especially first weeks — evening dosing helps), decreased blood pressure (dizziness on standing), decreased heart rate, headache, dry mouth, constipation.

Interactions: Other blood pressure medications — effects add. Avoid high-fat meals at dose time.

Catapres (clonidine)

How it works: Non-stimulant. Acts on alpha-2 adrenergic receptors in the brain and peripheral nervous system. Helps regulate arousal, reduce impulsivity, and improve sleep onset. No abuse potential.

Practical details: Titrated slowly. Do not stop suddenly — can cause rebound elevated blood pressure and heart rate. Always taper gradually. Alcohol significantly increases sedation — avoid or strictly limit.

What to expect: Useful for hyperarousal, impulsivity, and sleep difficulties. Sedation common initially. Low BP and dizziness (especially on standing) usually diminish after weeks at a stable dose.

Common side effects: Sedation (can be significant when starting), dizziness on standing, decreased blood pressure, decreased heart rate, dry mouth, constipation, headache, low mood or fatigue in some.

Interactions: Alcohol significantly increases sedation — avoid/limit. Other blood pressure medications — effects add. Inform all doctors you are taking it.

3Non-Pharmacological Strategies
Non-Pharmacological Approaches & Techniques

Psychological therapies:

  • CBT for ADHD (strong evidence): Targets procrastination, disorganisation, time management, avoidance, and negative self-beliefs. Skills-based and practical. ~60–70% show meaningful functional gains.
  • ACT — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (moderate–strong): Teaches psychological flexibility. Key techniques: defusion ("I notice I am having the thought that I am useless" rather than "I am useless"), values clarification, committed action. Particularly helpful for those exhausted by trying to control their mind.
  • DBT skills training (moderate–strong): Especially for emotional dysregulation and rejection sensitivity. Four modules — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness — map directly onto ADHD's most impairing features.
  • ADHD coaching (moderate): Practical and present-focused. Goal attainment, accountability, routine-building. Best for functionally stable people needing structured accountability. Look for ICF-accredited coaches with ADHD training.
  • Occupational therapy (emerging): Hands-on solutions — workspace organisation, routine scaffolding, sensory strategies, assistive technology. May be fundable through NDIS.

Practical strategies:

  • Body doubling: Working in the presence of another person — physically or via video — without necessarily collaborating. Many people with ADHD initiate and sustain tasks dramatically better when someone is simply present. Focusmate (focusmate.com) provides free online sessions.
  • Brain dump + priority matrix: Get everything out of your head onto a list — everything, regardless of size or urgency. Then sort by importance and urgency. Externalising the task list is itself calming.
  • Time blocking with visual timers: Allocate specific tasks to specific time slots. Pomodoro technique: 25 minutes on, 5-minute break. Time Timer (visual countdown as shrinking red disc) makes time passage concrete.
  • If-then planning: "If it is 9am and I sit at my desk, then I will open the document I need to work on first." More specific = more effective. Write it down.
  • Reward pairing: Listen to a favourite podcast only while doing routine tasks. Set a small, concrete reward for completing a defined block.
  • Exercise: A single session of aerobic exercise produces immediate, measurable improvement in attention, working memory, and impulse control for 1–4 hours — comparable to a low-dose stimulant. Aim for 20+ minutes aerobic, earlier in the day.
  • Decision fatigue: Automate recurring decisions — weekly meal structure, default answers to common requests. Do cognitively demanding work during your medication peak.
Reducing Phone & Social Media Use

Smartphones are engineered to capture attention. For ADHD brains, the goal is not willpower — it is friction. Make undesirable habits physically harder.

  • Hidden folders: Move Instagram/TikTok into a folder within a folder on the last page. Extra taps break the automatic reflex.
  • Grayscale mode: Settings → Accessibility → Colour Filters → Greyscale. Makes the phone boring. Works.
  • Screen Time limits: Set daily limits per app. Have someone else set the passcode if you override impulsively.
  • Brick: Physical NFC card (brickself.com) that locks apps for a set period.
  • Notification audit: Turn off all non-essential notifications. Every interruption costs 10–20 minutes of reorientation.
  • Charge outside bedroom: Eliminates late-night scrolling and morning phone-grab before medication kicks in.
Emotional Regulation Strategies

Emotional dysregulation in ADHD is neurological — the same prefrontal circuits managing attention also regulate emotional responses. Emotions arrive faster, feel more intense, and take longer to settle. This is not a character flaw; it is the same mechanism as the attention problems.

In the moment — when already activated:

  • TIPP (DBT): Temperature (submerge face in cold water 15–30 seconds — activates the mammalian dive reflex, rapidly lowers heart rate); Intense exercise (60 seconds of jumping jacks burns physiological activation); Paced breathing (exhale longer than inhale: 4 in, 7 out); Progressive muscle relaxation.
  • STOP skill (DBT): Stop. Take a step back physically if possible. Observe what you are feeling without acting on it. Proceed mindfully — even 30 seconds changes outcomes.
  • 90-second rule: The physiological component of an emotional response lasts approximately 90 seconds if you do not feed it with rumination. Ride the wave without acting and it naturally subsides.

Longer-term regulation:

  • PLEASE skills (DBT): Treat physical illness. Eat regularly. Avoid mood-altering substances. Sleep adequately. Exercise daily. These are prerequisites, not optional extras.
  • Opposite Action (DBT): When the emotion prompts an action that makes things worse, do the opposite. Shame says withdraw — opposite is to reach out.
  • Defusion (ACT): Create distance from intense thoughts. "I notice I am having the thought that I am worthless" instead of "I am worthless."
  • Emotion naming: Accurately labelling the feeling ("I feel rejected and ashamed" not just "bad") activates the prefrontal cortex and reduces amygdala reactivity.
  • Map RSD triggers in advance: Identify situations that reliably activate rejection sensitivity. Knowing the trigger in advance lets you apply skills before you are flooded.
Study Tips

Standard study advice is written for neurotypical brains. ADHD requires techniques that work with the interest-driven attention system.

  • Active recall, not re-reading: Read a section, close it, write everything you remember, then check. Flashcards with spaced repetition (Anki app) are ideal.
  • Remove the phone: Get a smartwatch and put your phone in another room. Non-negotiable.
  • Feynman Technique: Explain the concept aloud as if teaching someone. Where you get stuck is the exact gap. Fill it, then explain again.
  • Body doubling for study: Library, cafe, or Focusmate (virtual body doubling service).
  • Study during medication peak: Schedule demanding work 60–90 minutes after taking stimulant. Do not save it for when medication has worn off.
  • Interest bridging: Find the angle that connects the material to something you care about. ADHD brains learn through interest.
Binge Eating & Appetite

Binge eating is significantly more common in ADHD. The ADHD brain seeks dopamine; highly palatable food provides a reliable, immediate hit. Impulsivity reduces the pause between urge and action. Skipping meals (especially when stimulants suppress lunch appetite) drives hunger that accelerates loss of control later.

  • Eat regularly: Small, scheduled lunch — even if not hungry — prevents significant hunger driving evening overconsumption.
  • Reduce friction for healthy eating: Keep easy, adequate food accessible; make high-stimulation binge foods harder to reach.
  • Identify the emotional trigger: Binge urges often follow boredom, procrastination, frustration, or overwhelm — not just hunger.
  • Address the dopamine need directly: Movement, music, or a brief engaging task can reduce the urge without eating.
  • If significantly impacting life: Raise with your psychiatrist. Medication adjustments and/or psychological support (CBT-E or DBT) have good evidence.
Sleep & ADHD

Why ADHD and sleep do not mix: The ADHD brain struggles to disengage at bedtime — thoughts keep firing when you want to wind down. Delayed sleep phase (being a natural night owl) is more common in ADHD. Stimulant timing can also affect sleep onset.

What actually helps:

  • Bright light therapy: Morning bright light (10,000 lux light box or Re-Timer glasses worn for 30–60 minutes on waking) is one of the higher-yield sleep interventions in ADHD. It resets the circadian rhythm and makes falling asleep at night easier.
  • Consistent wake time — including weekends — anchors the circadian rhythm more effectively than a consistent bedtime.
  • Review stimulant timing with your psychiatrist if sleep onset is problematic.
  • Sleep hygiene basics: Cool, dark room; no screens 1 hour before bed; white noise if helpful.
  • Melatonin or clonidine can assist with sleep onset — discuss with your psychiatrist.
  • If sleep problems persist despite these measures, ask your GP about a formal sleep assessment.
Disclosing Your Diagnosis

You are never obligated to disclose your diagnosis. It is a personal decision with real trade-offs.

Before disclosing, consider:

  • Stigma remains real. Not everyone understands ADHD or believes in it. Some will see it as an excuse.
  • Disclosure does not guarantee protection or accommodation.
  • Some contexts are safer than others (close relationships vs workplace vs casual acquaintances).

When disclosure may help:

  • Requesting specific, practical accommodations that would meaningfully help your performance.
  • Partners and close family — understanding often significantly reduces relationship friction.
  • Trusted colleagues who can provide informal support or accountability.

If people are sceptical: You do not need to convince them. A simple response: "I have had a thorough assessment and I am comfortable with the diagnosis." You do not owe anyone a debate.

Workplace Adjustments

Disclosure: Not legally required. Disclose on a need-to-know basis only. Stigma remains real — proceed thoughtfully.

Useful adjustments: Quiet or people-filled workspace (choose what works), noise-cancelling headphones, standing desk, regular brief check-ins with manager, written task lists and clear deadlines.

Self-employed: Batch admin into one weekly block, automate invoicing, fixed daily start routine, external accountability via coach or colleague.

Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviours (BFRBs)

What they are: Skin picking, hair pulling (trichotillomania), nail biting, cheek chewing. Significantly more common with ADHD.

Why they occur: Stimulation-seeking, impulsivity, emotional regulation via habit loop, often automatic/unconscious.

What helps: Habit Reversal Training (most evidence-based), barrier strategies (pimple patches, gloves while driving), fidget substitutes, awareness training. ADHD treatment itself often reduces frequency.

Communication & ADHD

How ADHD affects communication: Impulsive interrupting, losing the conversational thread, emotional reactivity in conflict, inconsistency between intention and follow-through.

For the person with ADHD: Write things down immediately, ask for written summaries, time important conversations for when medication is active.

For partners/family: Short direct requests, written reminders, do not take forgetfulness personally, repair conflict quickly, assume positive intent.

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

Stimulants in pregnancy: Small increased risk of preterm birth and low birth weight. Usual approach: discuss stopping or reducing, especially first trimester, unless risks of untreated ADHD are significant.

Non-stimulants: Atomoxetine, guanfacine, clonidine — generally stopped in pregnancy due to limited safety data.

Breastfeeding: Small amounts pass into breast milk. Ritalin has lowest excretion and is generally preferred. Monitor infant for reduced feeding or irritability.

Key message: Individual decision with psychiatrist and obstetrician. Plan early. Non-pharmacological strategies become particularly important during this period.

5Women & Girls with ADHD
Why Women and Girls Are Underdiagnosed
The Diagnostic Gap

Women and girls with ADHD are diagnosed on average 5–10 years later than males, and many are never diagnosed at all. This is not because ADHD is rarer in females — prevalence is similar — but because the condition presents differently and the diagnostic criteria were historically developed from research on young boys.

  • Predominantly inattentive presentation is more common in girls — less visible, less disruptive, easier to miss
  • Girls are more likely to compensate through intense effort, people-pleasing, and masking — presenting as "coping" while exhausted internally
  • Internalising symptoms (anxiety, depression, low self-esteem) dominate the clinical picture and attract diagnostic attention first
  • Social motivation drives compliance — symptoms are less obvious to teachers in structured settings
  • Hyperactivity in girls is often internalised (racing thoughts, inner restlessness) rather than externalised movement
  • Teachers and parents are less likely to refer girls, even when symptoms are equivalent to boys
Why Late Diagnosis Matters
  • Years of unexplained underperformance → profound shame, self-blame, and identity damage
  • Diagnosis in adulthood often follows life events: new academic demands, first job, parenthood, relationship breakdown, perimenopause
  • Many women describe the diagnosis as "finally making sense of my whole life"
  • Higher rates of self-harm, eating disorders, and suicide attempts in undiagnosed women with ADHD
Presentation Differences in Women
  • Masking / camouflaging: Intense effort to appear neurotypical — mimicking peers, scripting conversations, exhausting social "performance." Leads to profound fatigue and burnout.
  • Emotional dysregulation: Often the dominant complaint — intense emotional reactions, RSD, mood swings. Frequently misdiagnosed as BPD or cyclothymia.
  • Perfectionism as compensation: Extremely high standards mask executive dysfunction — until demands exceed capacity, then sudden collapse.
  • People-pleasing and difficulty saying no: Impulsivity + fear of rejection → chronic overcommitment.
  • Internal hyperactivity: Racing thoughts, inability to switch off, constant inner commentary.
  • Chronic disorganisation as a private shame: Many women are visibly "together" publicly while their home/finances/inner life are chaotic.
  • Hyperfocus on relationships: Intense investment in friendships and romantic relationships; devastation when they end.
Comorbidity Pattern in Women
  • Higher rates of anxiety, depression, eating disorders (especially binge eating), BPD, PTSD, fibromyalgia
  • Emotional dysregulation often misattributed to BPD — ADHD neurological basis is key to accurate diagnosis
  • Self-harm and suicidality: ADHD is an independent risk factor — screen thoroughly
  • Burnout: particularly common in women who have masked for decades
Hormonal Influences on ADHD in Women
Oestrogen & Dopamine

Oestrogen potentiates dopaminergic function in the prefrontal cortex — the same system impaired in ADHD. This means fluctuations in oestrogen directly and significantly affect ADHD symptom severity.

Menstrual Cycle
  • ADHD symptoms commonly worsen premenstrually (late luteal phase) when oestrogen falls
  • Women may notice: increased emotional dysregulation, executive dysfunction, rejection sensitivity, difficulty with medication efficacy in the week before menstruation
  • Perimenstrual symptom worsening can look like PMDD — the two often co-occur but are distinct
  • Clinical response: may need temporary dose adjustment perimenstrually — document the pattern and review with psychiatrist
  • Combined oral contraceptive pill (stable oestrogen) may improve symptom consistency — discuss with GP/gynaecologist
Perimenopause & Menopause
  • Perimenopause: significant and often abrupt ADHD exacerbation as oestrogen begins declining (typically late 30s–50s)
  • Women who previously coped well may suddenly find ADHD symptoms debilitating — a common trigger for first diagnosis in midlife
  • Medication dose may need to increase during perimenopause
  • HRT (hormone replacement therapy) may improve ADHD symptom severity by stabilising oestrogen — not yet well-studied but consistent with mechanism
  • Post-menopausal: symptoms often stabilise at a new (higher) baseline — medication dose recalibration may be needed
Pregnancy & Postpartum
  • Pregnancy: oestrogen rises dramatically — some women find ADHD symptoms actually improve, others worsen
  • Postpartum: abrupt oestrogen drop + sleep deprivation + demands of newborn care → often severe ADHD exacerbation
  • Postpartum period is high-risk for first ADHD recognition in women — previously coped; now overwhelmed
  • Non-pharmacological management preferred in pregnancy and breastfeeding — see Pregnancy card
  • Plan ahead: discuss medication strategy before or early in pregnancy with psychiatrist + obstetrician
Prescribing Notes for Women
  • Contraception: no clinically significant pharmacokinetic interaction with ADHD stimulants
  • Document menstrual cycle tracking to identify perimenstrual symptom patterns
  • Lisdexamfetamine: dual PBS indication for ADHD and Binge Eating Disorder — particularly relevant in women
  • In perimenopause: coordinate with GP regarding HRT and ADHD medication — both may need adjustment
  • Discuss contraception plans before starting medications in women of childbearing age
Girls with ADHD — Developmental Considerations
Why Girls Are Missed
  • Girls' ADHD symptoms are often attributed to personality ("she's a daydreamer," "she's emotional," "she's sensitive")
  • Academic masking through anxiety-driven perfectionism: grades may look fine while the effort required is unsustainable
  • Teachers less likely to refer girls — classroom behaviour is often compliant even when internally overwhelmed
  • Social consequences are significant: girls depend more on social cohesion, and social difficulties from ADHD can be devastating
  • Adolescence is the peak crisis point: puberty brings hormonal fluctuation + increasing academic demands + social complexity → masking capacity collapses
Clinical Presentation in Adolescent Girls
  • Anxiety and depression as the presenting complaint (underlying ADHD often missed)
  • Eating disorders (restrictive or binge/purge) — ADHD impulsivity + dopamine-seeking drives disordered eating
  • Self-harm — emotional dysregulation + impulsivity + shame
  • Social difficulties: intense friendships followed by explosive endings; rejection sensitivity prominent
  • Academic "potential vs performance" gap: teachers note she could do better "if she tried"
  • Hyperfixations on interests that may be mistaken for typical teen behaviour
Assessment Tips
  • Ask directly about internal experience — not just external behaviour
  • Explore masking: "How much effort does it take to appear organised/calm?"
  • Get collateral from parents and ideally a teacher — girl may appear composed in the appointment
  • Screen for eating disorders, self-harm, and anxiety as standard
Masking, Burnout & Identity in Women with ADHD
What is Masking?

Masking is the process of suppressing, camouflaging, or compensating for ADHD symptoms to appear neurotypical. It is learned — often unconsciously — as a survival strategy in a world that penalises deviation from expected behaviour.

Common masking strategies include:

  • Studying peers and mimicking social behaviour
  • Scripting conversations in advance
  • Over-apologising to pre-empt perceived failure
  • Extreme organisation systems to hide executive dysfunction
  • Suppressing emotional reactions at enormous energy cost
  • People-pleasing to avoid perceived rejection

The cost of masking: It works — until it doesn't. Chronic masking is extraordinarily energy-intensive. It often produces a public persona of competence alongside private collapse: missed meals, chaotic home environments, exhaustion, and profound shame about the gap between how one appears and how one feels.

ADHD Burnout

ADHD burnout occurs when the cumulative load of managing unrecognised ADHD symptoms — masking, compensating, overcommitting — exceeds capacity. It is distinct from depression, though the two overlap.

Features of ADHD burnout:

  • Profound physical and cognitive exhaustion
  • Inability to mask — symptoms suddenly visible where they were previously hidden
  • Emotional flatness or emotional flooding, alternating
  • Withdrawal from responsibilities and relationships
  • Loss of capacity that seemed intact before

Recovery requires: Rest (not productivity), reduction of demands, removal of masking pressure, medication review, and often psychological support specifically for ADHD.

Identity & Diagnosis

Many women describe ADHD diagnosis as both a relief and a grief — relief at finally understanding themselves, grief for the years of mislabelling, shame, and paths not taken. The diagnostic process should honour both experiences. Psychoeducation about ADHD as a neurological difference — not a character flaw — is core to recovery.

  • Stimulants often less effective and produce more side effects in ASD
  • Start at half the usual starting dose; titrate more slowly
  • More irritability, emotional dysregulation, and appetite suppression reported
  • Non-stimulants (atomoxetine, guanfacine) often better tolerated
  • Guanfacine: particularly useful for irritability, aggression, and hyperactivity in ASD
  • Behavioural interventions and OT are crucial — do not rely on medication alone
5Substance Use Disorder
ADHD with Substance Use Disorder (SUD)
Epidemiology & Rationale for Treatment
  • 25–50% of adults with SUD meet ADHD criteria
  • ADHD is a significant risk factor for developing SUD
  • Treating ADHD may reduce substance use behaviours
  • If possible: assess ADHD after a period of sobriety (ideally ≥1 month) to distinguish substance-induced symptoms from genuine ADHD
  • Do not defer ADHD treatment indefinitely — untreated ADHD maintains SUD cycle
Prescribing in Active SUD
  • Preferred: Lisdexamfetamine (prodrug; significantly lower abuse potential)
  • Consider: Atomoxetine (non-stimulant; no misuse potential) as first-line if stimulant SUD
  • Avoid: IR stimulants (dexamphetamine IR, MPH IR) in active stimulant use disorder
  • Long-acting preparations always preferred over IR
  • Regular RTPM checks; shorter prescription intervals; smaller quantities
  • Urine drug screening (periodic; via GP)
  • Coordinate with addiction medicine / drug and alcohol service
  • Prescribe with clear parameters for cessation if diversion detected

Paediatric ADHD — Key Differences
Assessment Differences
  • Teacher ratings are mandatory (Vanderbilt, Conners-3 teacher form)
  • Parent interview is the primary source (not child self-report alone)
  • School reports / developmental records highly valuable
  • Consider neuropsychological testing if learning disorder overlap
  • Rule out hearing/vision impairment in young children
  • Assess family functioning and parenting — both affect symptom severity
Telehealth Considerations in Children
  • Parent may need to be present initially to assist with technology and rapport
  • Observe child's behaviour during the video call (attentiveness, activity level, impulsivity)
  • School-age children: morning sessions when medication may have worn off → observe unmedicated state
  • Collateral from school via written reports or separate consultation (item 91874–91878)
Pharmacotherapy in Children (≥6 years)
  • First-line: Methylphenidate IR or LA (most evidence, TGA-approved)
  • Second-line: Lisdexamfetamine (TGA-approved ≥6 years); dexamphetamine
  • Non-stimulants: Atomoxetine (4–12 weeks onset); Guanfacine XR (PBS-listed 6–17 years)
  • Weight-based dosing: 0.3–1 mg/kg/day methylphenidate (start 0.3, max 1 mg/kg or 60 mg)
  • Monitor height and weight every 3–6 months; plot on growth chart
  • Consider medication holidays (school holidays) if growth suppression is significant
  • Parent Management Training (PMT) is first-line for preschool children and important for all ages
  • School-based accommodations: extended time, preferential seating, movement breaks
Preschool children (<6 years) Non-pharmacological treatment (parent management training, behavioural interventions) is the recommended first-line approach. Methylphenidate may be considered in preschool children only when behavioural interventions have been optimised and ADHD is severely impairing — specialist decision only.
7Non-Pharmacological Management
Evidence-Based Non-Pharmacological Interventions
Psychoeducation (Essential — All Patients)
  • Neurobiological model of ADHD — reduces shame
  • Executive function deficits — working memory, inhibition, time blindness
  • ADHD as a lifelong trait, not a character flaw
  • Impact on relationships, work, self-esteem
  • Medication as a tool, not a cure
  • Resources: ADHD Australia, CHADD (international), AADPA guidelines
CBT for ADHD
  • Structured, skills-based (not insight-based like traditional CBT)
  • Targets: time management, organisation, procrastination, emotional regulation
  • Can be delivered via telehealth effectively
  • Programs: "Mastering Your Adult ADHD" (Safren); "ADHD in Adults" (Bramham)
ADHD Coaching
  • Goal-oriented; practical skills; real-world accountability
  • Telephone / video coaching highly effective
  • Complements medication well
Lifestyle — Strong Evidence
  • Exercise: 20–30 min aerobic daily → equivalent to low-dose stimulant acutely. Improves dopamine, executive function, mood.
  • Sleep: Critical — poor sleep dramatically worsens all ADHD symptoms. Prioritise sleep hygiene before adjusting medication dose.
  • Diet: Regular meals; avoid blood glucose fluctuations; omega-3 supplementation (modest evidence)
  • Mindfulness: Moderate evidence for attention and emotion regulation
Environmental Strategies
  • External systems (calendars, reminders, apps) — don't rely on working memory
  • Reduce environmental distractors (noise-cancelling headphones, clear workspace)
  • Time-boxing and structured routines
  • Workplace / study accommodations (extended deadlines, quiet rooms)
  • Body doubling (working alongside others — in person or virtual)
Relationships & Family
  • ADHD-informed couple therapy if relationship strain is significant
  • Partner psychoeducation — essential for understanding and reducing blame
  • Parent management training (children with ADHD)
  • Family therapy where indicated

1How We Assess for ADHD
The Clinical Approach — What We're Actually Looking For

ADHD is a trait, not a state. The key question is not "do you have symptoms today?" but "have you always been this way, across most of your life, in most of your contexts?" We're looking for a lifelong pattern of executive dysregulation — not a recent change, not a situational response to stress, not something that appeared last year.

Interest-based attention. The ADHD brain runs on interest, urgency, novelty, and challenge — not importance alone. When something genuinely captures their interest, they can hyperfocus brilliantly for hours. When something is dull, repetitive, or lacks a proximate deadline, starting it can feel genuinely impossible — not laziness, but a different motivational architecture. This is diagnostic gold when you hear it.

Masking and late diagnosis. Many intelligent adults were never identified. They compensated through school via intelligence, anxiety-driven perfectionism, or a highly structured environment someone else provided. The diagnosis often surfaces when supports drop away — leaving school, losing a partner, starting a job with less external structure. Always explore: "Were you coping because of your own systems, or because someone or something else was organising you?"

Trait not state — practical heuristic. ADHD symptoms should be present across a wide range of contexts. If they only appear in one relationship, one job, or one life stage, consider whether something else explains the picture. True ADHD is pervasive — though it may be more obvious in some settings than others.

Assessment Heuristics — Patterns That Make the Diagnosis More Likely
Strong positive signals
  • Consistent lifelong pattern across multiple settings (home, school, work, relationships)
  • Interest-based attention: hyperfocus on engaging tasks, shut-down on boring ones
  • "Bright but not applying themselves" — teacher comments, report card themes
  • Internal restlessness, not just external — in adults, hyperactivity internalises
  • Time blindness: time exists as "now" vs "not now" — chronic lateness, missed deadlines
  • Rejection sensitive dysphoria — intense emotional response to perceived criticism
  • First-degree relative with ADHD (heritability ~76%)
  • Things improved substantially in structured environments or with urgency/novelty
  • Compensated until a major life transition removed their external scaffolding
Features that should prompt reconsideration
  • Onset clearly after a discrete life event or trauma (consider PTSD, adjustment)
  • Symptoms only in one setting or one relationship
  • Clear episodic pattern — symptoms much better between episodes (consider mood disorder)
  • Attention hijacked by worry, not wandering (consider anxiety disorder)
  • Concentration improves significantly when anxiety or mood is treated
  • Symptoms track sleep quality almost perfectly (consider primary sleep disorder)
  • Requesting only one specific medication by name, reluctant to consider alternatives
  • No functional impairment despite reported severe symptoms

The Diagnostic Standard — What Makes a Valid Assessment

AADPA 2024 standard: A valid ADHD assessment requires comprehensive psychiatric interview covering all DSM-5-TR criteria, developmental history, functional impairment, differential diagnosis, psychiatric comorbidities, medical history, substance use, MSE, and collateral information where available.

Rating scales (ASRS, DIVA) support but do not replace clinical interview. A positive ASRS alone does not make a diagnosis. A negative ASRS does not exclude ADHD in a high-masking individual.

Minimum collateral standard: In adults, collateral from a partner, parent, or close other is strongly preferred. In children, teacher and parent ratings are mandatory (Criterion C requires cross-situational evidence). For adults without available collateral, document the attempt and rely more heavily on developmental history exploration and school records.

Telehealth-specific note You cannot measure BP/HR, assess cardiac auscultation, or perform neurological exam via video. Request GP to perform baseline cardiovascular examination and observations before first prescription. Advise patient to check BP/HR at pharmacy or with home device at each titration step.

Criteria B–E — Context, Impairment, and Exclusions
B
Developmental origin (pre-age 12) — Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms were present before age 12 years. Note: symptoms do not need to have been formally identified before age 12; retrospective account is acceptable.
C

D

E
Differential exclusion — The symptoms do not occur exclusively during the course of schizophrenia or another psychotic disorder and are not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., mood disorder, anxiety disorder, dissociative disorder, personality disorder, substance intoxication or withdrawal).
