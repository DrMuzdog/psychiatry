ADHD Telehealth Reference Guide by Dr Benjamin Murrie · AADPA 2024
1Telehealth Setup
Pre-Session Checklist
Before the Appointment
  • Send screening questionnaires ≥48 h before (ASRS, DIVA-5, PHQ-9, GAD-7, AUDIT-C)
  • Request GP referral letter, previous reports, school records
  • Confirm patient has a private, quiet space for the session
  • Verify platform is encrypted and clinically compliant
  • Confirm patient's physical location (state/territory) — determines prescribing jurisdiction
  • Check RTPM / SafeScript before session starts
  • Consider whether collateral informant (partner, parent) should attend
Technical Minimums
  • Encrypted video platform (not standard Zoom for clinical care)
  • Patient needs stable internet, camera, microphone
  • Ensure you can see patient's face clearly for MSE
  • Phone as backup — but phone-only has more MBS restrictions
  • Document platform used and connection quality
  • Telehealth consent: verbal at minimum, written preferred; document in notes
Jurisdiction note S8 scripts must be written for the state/territory the patient is physically located in. Ask at session start.
What Telehealth Cannot Do — Plan Accordingly
Cannot Assess Remotely
  • Accurate BP & HR — request from patient's GP or pharmacy, or home device
  • Height and weight — self-report or from GP; essential for children
  • Cardiac auscultation (ECG via GP if indicated)
  • Full neurological examination
  • Direct observation of motor tics at rest
Can Assess Via Video
  • Appearance, grooming, eye contact, psychomotor activity
  • Speech rate, volume, fluency, latency
  • Mood and affect (verbal and facial expression)
  • Thought form and content
  • Insight and judgment
  • Observable restlessness, fidgeting, distractibility
Practical approach Request GP to perform cardiovascular examination and baseline observations before first prescription. Advise patient to have BP/HR checked at pharmacy or own device before titration reviews.

3Clinical Interview Framework
Structured Assessment — Recommended Order
1
Presenting complaint & reason for assessmentFunctional impairment driving referral. Work, study, relationships, self-management. Patient's own words first.
2
Current core symptoms (Criterion A)Review ASRS/DIVA responses. Probe each DSM-5-TR symptom. Ask for specific examples in current life. Rate severity (mild/moderate/severe) and frequency.
3
Developmental history — childhood onset (Criterion B)Symptoms before age 12. School performance (primary school is key). Reports, teacher comments, parent recollections. WURS responses. Ask: "Were you told you were restless/dreamy/couldn't concentrate at school?"
4
Cross-situational evidence (Criterion C)Which settings are affected: home, work, social, driving, finances, relationships? Need ≥2 settings. Ask about each domain explicitly.
5
Functional impairment (Criterion D)Quantify: job loss, relationship breakdown, academic failure, financial difficulties, near-miss accidents, self-esteem impact.
6
Differential diagnosis screenAnxiety, depression, bipolar, ASD, trauma, sleep disorder, substance use. Do not skip — these are both differentials AND common comorbidities.
7
Psychiatric historyPrior diagnoses, treatments, hospitalisations. Previous ADHD assessments or trials of medication.
8
Substance use historyAlcohol, cannabis, stimulants, opioids. Current use pattern. AUDIT. ADHD + SUD is common — affects medication choice.
9
Medical history & medicationsCardiac conditions, hypertension, tics, seizures, sleep disorders, liver disease. Current medications (interactions). Allergies.
10
Family historyADHD in first-degree relatives (heritability ~76%). Also: bipolar disorder, psychosis, substance use, tics/Tourette syndrome.
11
Social historyLiving situation, relationships, occupation, support network, driving, legal history.
12
MSE via videoAppearance, behaviour, speech, mood/affect, thought (form + content), perception, cognition (concentration/attention observable during interview), insight, judgment.
Example Probing Questions by Domain
Inattention
  • "Do you often re-read sentences without taking in what they say?"
  • "Do you often make careless mistakes in your work or miss details?"
  • "How do you manage to-do lists, bills, appointments?"
  • "Do you often start tasks but find it hard to follow through?"
  • "Do people say you don't seem to listen when they're talking to you?"
  • "Do you frequently lose keys, phone, wallet?"
  • "Does external noise or movement easily break your concentration?"
Childhood Onset
  • "At primary school, did teachers comment you were dreamy, chatty, or couldn't sit still?"
  • "Did you find it hard to complete homework? Did parents or teachers have to help more than most kids?"
  • "Did you often lose or forget things at school?"
  • "Were you ever held back a year or receive extra support?"
Hyperactivity / Impulsivity
  • "Do you feel restless or find it hard to sit still for long periods?"
  • "Do you often talk too much or over others in conversation?"
  • "Do you blurt out answers or finish other people's sentences?"
  • "Do you make impulsive decisions — purchases, job changes, relationships?"
  • "Do you find queuing or waiting very difficult?"
  • "Do you often interrupt in meetings or social settings?"
Functional Impact
  • "How has this affected your career or education?"
  • "Has it affected your relationships — partner, friends, family?"
  • "Do you have trouble managing money or paying bills on time?"
  • "Any driving incidents — fines, accidents, near-misses?"
  • "How does this affect your self-esteem?"
1DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria
Criterion A — Core Symptoms (must meet threshold in ≥1 domain)
Threshold Age ≤16: ≥6 symptoms in the domain(s). Age ≥17: ≥5 symptoms in the domain(s). Symptoms must have persisted for ≥6 months to a degree inconsistent with developmental level and negatively impacting social/academic/occupational activities.
Inattention (9 symptoms)
  • 1
    Often fails to give close attention to details, or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, work, or other activities
  • 2
    Often has difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play activities
  • 3
    Often does not seem to listen when spoken to directly
  • 4
    Often does not follow through on instructions and fails to finish tasks
  • 5
    Often has difficulty organising tasks and activities
  • 6
    Often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to engage in tasks requiring sustained mental effort
  • 7
    Often loses things necessary for tasks or activities
  • 8
    Is often easily distracted by extraneous stimuli
  • 9
    Is often forgetful in daily activities
Hyperactivity-Impulsivity (9 symptoms)
  • 1
    Often fidgets with or taps hands/feet, or squirms in seat
  • 2
    Often leaves seat when remaining seated is expected
  • 3
    Often runs about or climbs in inappropriate situations (adults: often feels restless)
  • 4
    Often unable to play or engage in leisure activities quietly
  • 5
    Is often "on the go," acting as if "driven by a motor"
  • 6
    Often talks excessively
  • 7
    Often blurts out an answer before a question has been completed
  • 8
    Often has difficulty waiting their turn
  • 9
    Often interrupts or intrudes on others
Criteria B–E & Specifiers
Remaining Criteria
  • B
    Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms were present before age 12 years
  • C
    Symptoms are present in two or more settings (home, school, work, with friends, other activities)
  • D
    Clear evidence that symptoms interfere with, or reduce the quality of social, academic, or occupational functioning
  • E
    Symptoms do not occur exclusively during schizophrenia or another psychotic disorder, and are not better explained by another mental disorder

  • Moderate Symptoms/impairment between mild and severe
  • Severe Many symptoms beyond threshold; marked functional impairment
2Differential Diagnosis
Key Differentials — Clinical Distinguishing Features
ConditionOverlapping featuresKey distinguishing features
Anxiety DisordersPoor concentration, restlessness, avoidance of tasksWorry is the driver of inattention (not wandering mind); restlessness is tension/dread-based; typically episodic. ADHD inattention is pervasive, lifelong, and consistent across situations.
Major DepressionPoor concentration, fatigue, low motivation, executive dysfunctionOnset correlates with depressive episode; concentration returns with remission. ADHD is chronic and present even when euthymic.
Bipolar DisorderImpulsivity, distractibility, racing thoughts, increased activity, talkativenessBipolar: episodic, with periods of normality. ADHD: persistent, continuous since childhood. Family history, sleep changes, and grandiosity distinguish bipolar hypomanic/manic states.
ASDInattention (to non-special-interest topics), social difficulties, rigidity, sensory sensitivityASD inattention is domain-specific (hyperfocus on special interests, inattention to non-interests). Social difficulties are qualitatively different. Can be comorbid — DSM-5 allows dual diagnosis.
Borderline PDImpulsivity, emotional lability, interpersonal difficulties, self-harmBPD: emotion regulation as primary driver; identity disturbance; interpersonal hypersensitivity; trauma history common. Can be comorbid with ADHD.
PTSD / Complex TraumaConcentration difficulties, hyperarousal, hypervigilance, emotional dysregulationPTSD: onset after trauma; hypervigilance is threat-driven not novelty-seeking. However, ADHD increases trauma risk and both commonly co-occur.
Sleep DisordersInattention, irritability, impulsivity, fatigue, poor executive functionOSA: daytime somnolence, snoring, witnessed apnoeas. Insomnia: can co-occur with and worsen ADHD. Always screen sleep — treat sleep disorders first or concurrently.
Substance Use DisordersImpulsivity, inattention, poor functioning, mood instabilitySUD symptoms may remit with sobriety. However ADHD co-occurs in ~30–50% of adults with SUD. If possible, assess after a period of sobriety.
Thyroid dysfunctionRestlessness (hyper), cognitive slowing (hypo), anxietyThyroid function tests rule in/out. Hyperthyroidism can mimic ADHD-HI.
Learning DisordersAcademic underperformance, task avoidance, frustrationLearning disorder: difficulty is domain-specific (reading, maths). ADHD: impairment is broad and context-dependent. Often co-occur.
3Common Comorbidities
Comorbidity Prevalence & Clinical Implications
ComorbidityPrevalence in ADHDClinical note
Anxiety disorders~50% adultsTreat ADHD first; stimulants may worsen anxiety in some — monitor. Atomoxetine has anxiolytic properties.
Major depression~20–30%ADHD chronically impairs function → secondary depression. Treat both. Stimulants alone may improve mood via treating ADHD.
ASD~20–50% of ADHD have ASD features; ~50–70% of ASD meet ADHD criteriaDual diagnosis is valid per DSM-5. Stimulants less effective and more side effects in ASD — start low, go slow.
Bipolar disorder~20%Carefully distinguish. If genuine comorbidity, mood stabiliser first, then consider non-stimulant or cautious stimulant under cover.
SUD (alcohol/drugs)~25–50% adultsPrefer LDX (prodrug) or non-stimulants. Avoid IR stimulants in active stimulant SUD. Treating ADHD may reduce SUD severity.
ODD / Conduct disorder~60% children with ADHDParent management training essential. Stimulants reduce ODD symptoms in ADHD. Guanfacine helpful for aggression.
Tic disorders / Tourette~20% ADHD have ticsStimulants may worsen tics in some — use lowest effective dose. Guanfacine/clonidine have anti-tic effects. Atomoxetine is tic-neutral.
Sleep disorders>50%Insomnia, delayed sleep phase, restless legs. Address sleep actively — poor sleep amplifies all ADHD symptoms.
Eating disordersElevated, especially binge eatingLisdexamfetamine is PBS-approved for binge eating disorder (BED). ADHD impulsivity drives binge eating.

4Side Effects & Monitoring
Side Effect Reference — Stimulants
Common (>10%)
  • Decreased appetite (usually most prominent mid-morning to afternoon)
  • Weight loss (dose-dependent; significant in children)
  • Insomnia / sleep onset delay (timing-dependent — dose too late)
  • Headache (usually resolves in first 1–2 weeks)
  • Dry mouth
  • Nausea (take with food)
  • BP increase (~2–4 mmHg systolic average)
  • HR increase (~2–4 bpm average)
  • Irritability / emotional lability (often at peak concentration or "rebound")
Uncommon / Serious
  • Anxiety exacerbation (especially in anxiety-prone individuals)
  • Mood elevation / mania (risk in undiagnosed bipolar)
  • Psychotic symptoms (risk in vulnerable individuals)
  • Tic emergence or worsening (reassess — usually manageable)
  • Growth suppression (children — monitor height/weight 6-monthly)
  • Rebound effect (irritability, tearfulness as dose wears off)
  • Cardiovascular events (very rare; mainly in pre-existing disease)
  • Priapism (very rare)
  • Substance misuse / diversion
Telehealth monitoring BP/HR cannot be directly measured. Instruct patients to check BP at pharmacy or use home device before each titration review. Get baseline from GP before starting.

1Australian State & Territory Requirements
State-by-State Prescribing Authorities — Stimulants (Adult ADHD)
Key principle for telehealth S8 prescriptions must comply with the laws of the state/territory where the patient is physically located at the time of the consultation, not where the clinician is located. Confirm patient location at start of each session.
NSW
SafeScript NSW — mandatory check before every S8 script. Psychiatrists: prescribe without prior approval. Max doses: dexamphetamine 60 mg/day, methylphenidate 60 mg/day, lisdexamfetamine 70 mg/day. Exceeding these requires Individual Patient Authority from NSW Ministry of Health. Controlled Medicines Patient Treatment Register (CMPRT) if high-risk.
VIC
SafeScript VIC — mandatory. Psychiatrists: prescribe directly. Max: dexamphetamine 60 mg, methylphenidate 80 mg (authority for >60 mg), lisdexamfetamine 70 mg. Authority required from Drugs, Poisons and Controlled Substances Unit for higher doses or complex cases.
QLD
QScript — mandatory. Psychiatrists: prescribe under authority — Queensland Health's Stimulant Prescribing Reference Guide. Max: dexamphetamine 60 mg, methylphenidate 60 mg, lisdexamfetamine 70 mg. Private patients require private authority approval. PBS items require separate authority application (PRODA).
WA
ScriptCheckWA — mandatory. UDS mandatory before initiating treatment in adults ≥16 years. Psychiatrists: can prescribe for ADHD. Max: dexamphetamine 60 mg, methylphenidate 60 mg, lisdexamfetamine 70 mg. DAA / DOHWA oversight. Higher doses require WA DoH authority.
SA
ScriptCheckSA — mandatory. Psychiatrists: prescribe directly. SA Drug and Alcohol Services (DASSA) notification may be required for complex cases. Max: dexamphetamine 60 mg, methylphenidate 60 mg, lisdexamfetamine 70 mg.
TAS
TasScript — mandatory. Psychiatrists: can prescribe directly. Tasmanian Controlled Medicines framework. Standard dose caps as per other states. Report to TGA Adverse Event system if unusual reactions.
ACT
Canberra Script — mandatory. Psychiatrists: prescribe directly under ACT Medicines, Poisons and Therapeutic Goods Act. Max: standard per national guidelines. Similar framework to NSW.
NT
NTScript — mandatory. Additional requirements apply for remote and Aboriginal communities. Psychiatrists: prescribe under NT Poisons and Dangerous Drugs Act. Max doses as per national guidelines.
PBS Authority & eScripting
PBS Authority Process (PRODA / phone)
  • All ADHD stimulants require PBS Authority — apply via PRODA (online) or Services Australia (phone)
  • Authority number must appear on script
  • PRODA online: fastest option — real-time for most ADHD items
  • Telephone authority: 1800 888 333 (business hours)
  • Document authority number in patient notes
  • Authority is for the specific medication, dose range, and indication
Total tablets per day calculation
  • PBS calculates supply based on total tabs/day × 20 days = qty per script
  • Dex 5 mg: 12 tabs/day max → 240 tabs for 20 days
  • Repeat interval: minimum 20 days between fills for S8 stimulants (most states)
  • Repeats: varies by state. Document number of repeats on script.
eScripting for S8
  • Electronic prescribing (eScript) is now available for S8 medications in most states
  • Patient receives token via SMS or email → dispenses at pharmacy
  • Particularly useful in telehealth — no paper script to mail
  • Confirm state allows eScript for S8 — most now do
  • RTPM check required regardless of whether eScript or paper
Private Scripts
  • Non-PBS stimulants (e.g. higher doses, off-label): write as private S8
  • Must include all mandatory S8 script requirements per state
  • Clonidine, guanfacine (adults): private S4 script
  • Atomoxetine: PBS listed; does not require S8 authority
Contraindications & Cautions for Stimulant Prescribing
Absolute Contraindications (stimulants)
  • Structural cardiac disease with significant arrhythmia risk
  • Severe hypertension (uncontrolled)
  • Glaucoma (narrow-angle)
  • Hyperthyroidism (untreated)
  • Active psychotic disorder (or within past 12 months)
  • MAOIs (within 14 days)
  • Known hypersensitivity to amphetamines/methylphenidate
Cautions — Proceed Carefully / Consider Non-Stimulant
  • Tics / Tourette syndrome (monitor; may exacerbate in some)
  • Active substance use disorder (prefer LDX; avoid IR agents)
  • Bipolar disorder (mood stabiliser cover preferred first)
  • History of psychosis (careful risk-benefit; monitoring)
  • Anxiety disorders (start low; may worsen)
  • Borderline hypertension — baseline >135/85 mmHg
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding
  • Severe hepatic impairment (especially atomoxetine)
  • Raynaud phenomenon

What to Cover at Every Follow-up (Telehealth Checklist)
  • Efficacy: Is the ADHD better? Which domains? Global impression.
  • Coverage: Does it last long enough across the day?
  • Adherence: Taking as prescribed? Missing doses?
  • Side effects: Appetite, sleep, mood, anxiety, cardiovascular symptoms
  • BP/HR: Has patient measured? Review numbers.
  • Sleep: Onset, duration, quality. Timing of last dose.
  • Mood: Any depression, anxiety, irritability, elevated mood?
  • Substance use: Screen periodically. Any change?
  • Function: Work, study, relationships improving?
  • RTPM check: Mandatory before signing S8 script
  • Prescription: Adequate supply? Date last script filled?
  • Plan: Document treatment decision clearly (keep same / up-titrate / down-titrate / switch / cease)

GP Handover Letter — Required Elements
  • Diagnosis: DSM-5-TR subtype, severity specifier, ICD-10 code, date established
  • Diagnostic basis: Brief formulation — screening tools used, developmental history, functional impairment, criteria met
  • Comorbidities: List current and relevant past psychiatric and medical diagnoses
  • Current medication and dose: Drug, dose, timing, formulation, number of repeats, interval
  • Optimal dose reached: State the maintenance dose clearly
  • Prescribing authority: State-specific S8 authority for GP (varies by state — include relevant guidance)
  • Monitoring requirements: BP/HR frequency, weight (if child), RTPM check every script, annual review
  • Side effects to watch: Which ones, what action to take if seen
  • When to refer back: Loss of efficacy, significant side effects, new psychiatric symptoms, dose limit reached, patient request
  • Your contact: For GP queries
  • Non-pharmacological strategies discussed
  • Cessation protocol (if applicable — especially guanfacine/clonidine: must taper)

1Women & Girls
Female Presentation — Clinical Pearls
Why Women Are Underdiagnosed
  • Predominantly inattentive presentation is more common in girls (less visible hyperactivity)
  • Compensatory masking — high effort to "keep up" conceals deficits at school
  • Diagnosis often delayed until mid-adulthood when masking strategies fail (career, parenthood)
  • Internalising symptoms (anxiety, depression) often dominate the clinical picture
  • Social motivation drives compliance — symptoms less obvious to teachers
Comorbidity Pattern in Women
  • Higher rates of anxiety, depression, eating disorders, borderline PD
  • ADHD often secondary to these presentations — dig deeper if multiple comorbidities
  • Emotional dysregulation prominent (often misattributed to BPD or cyclothymia)
  • Self-harm and suicidality: ADHD is an independent risk factor — screen thoroughly
Hormonal Influences
  • Oestrogen potentiates dopaminergic function — fluctuations alter ADHD severity
  • ADHD symptoms commonly worsen premenstrually (low oestrogen phase)
  • Perimenopause: significant ADHD exacerbation common as oestrogen declines
  • May need dose increase perimenstrually or perimenopausal period
  • HRT may reduce ADHD symptom severity in perimenopause (not well-studied)
Prescribing Considerations
  • Contraception: no significant pharmacokinetic interaction with stimulants
  • Pregnancy: see pregnancy section — non-pharmacological strongly preferred
  • Lisdexamfetamine: dual indication for ADHD + BED — relevant in women with both
  • Discuss contraception plans before starting medications in women of childbearing age

3Older Adults (≥65)
ADHD in Older Adults
Diagnostic Challenges
  • Must distinguish from early dementia / mild cognitive impairment (MCI)
  • ADHD: lifelong course; cognitive decline in dementia is progressive and differs in profile
  • Depression and anxiety are major confounders — treat first and reassess
  • Many older adults present late — decades of undiagnosed ADHD with significant impairment history
  • Neuropsychological testing helpful when diagnosis uncertain
Prescribing Considerations
  • Start low, go slow — higher sensitivity to cardiovascular side effects
  • Polypharmacy risk — check all interactions carefully
  • Methylphenidate generally preferred over amphetamines in older adults (better tolerability data)
  • Lower target doses: often 10–20 mg methylphenidate or 20–30 mg LDX
  • More frequent BP/HR monitoring (every 2–4 weeks during titration)
  • Renal and hepatic function may affect drug clearance
  • Atomoxetine requires dose adjustment in hepatic impairment
4ASD + ADHD
Co-occurring ASD and ADHD
Epidemiology & Diagnosis
  • 50–70% of individuals with ASD meet criteria for ADHD
  • 20–50% of individuals with ADHD have ASD features
  • DSM-5 (2013) removed the exclusion criterion — dual diagnosis now valid
  • ASD inattention is qualitatively different: domain-specific, driven by cognitive style, not executive dysregulation
  • Comprehensive ASD assessment needed before dual diagnosis — consider neuropsychology
Pharmacotherapy in ASD+ADHD
  • Stimulants often less effective and produce more side effects in ASD
  • Start at half the usual starting dose; titrate more slowly
  • More irritability, emotional dysregulation, and appetite suppression reported
  • Non-stimulants (atomoxetine, guanfacine) often better tolerated
  • Guanfacine: particularly useful for irritability, aggression, and hyperactivity in ASD
  • Behavioural interventions and OT are crucial — do not rely on medication alone
5Substance Use Disorder
ADHD with Substance Use Disorder (SUD)
Epidemiology & Rationale for Treatment
  • 25–50% of adults with SUD meet ADHD criteria
  • ADHD is a significant risk factor for developing SUD
  • Treating ADHD may reduce substance use behaviours
  • If possible: assess ADHD after a period of sobriety (ideally ≥1 month) to distinguish substance-induced symptoms from genuine ADHD
  • Do not defer ADHD treatment indefinitely — untreated ADHD maintains SUD cycle
Prescribing in Active SUD
  • Preferred: Lisdexamfetamine (prodrug; significantly lower abuse potential)
  • Consider: Atomoxetine (non-stimulant; no misuse potential) as first-line if stimulant SUD
  • Avoid: IR stimulants (dexamphetamine IR, MPH IR) in active stimulant use disorder
  • Long-acting preparations always preferred over IR
  • Regular RTPM checks; shorter prescription intervals; smaller quantities
  • Urine drug screening (periodic; via GP)
  • Coordinate with addiction medicine / drug and alcohol service
  • Prescribe with clear parameters for cessation if diversion detected
6Children & Adolescents
Paediatric ADHD — Key Differences
Assessment Differences
  • Teacher ratings are mandatory (Vanderbilt, Conners-3 teacher form)
  • Parent interview is the primary source (not child self-report alone)
  • School reports / developmental records highly valuable
  • Consider neuropsychological testing if learning disorder overlap
  • Rule out hearing/vision impairment in young children
  • Assess family functioning and parenting — both affect symptom severity
Telehealth Considerations in Children
  • Parent may need to be present initially to assist with technology and rapport
  • Observe child's behaviour during the video call (attentiveness, activity level, impulsivity)
  • School-age children: morning sessions when medication may have worn off → observe unmedicated state
  • Collateral from school via written reports or separate consultation (item 91874–91878)
Pharmacotherapy in Children (≥6 years)
  • First-line: Methylphenidate IR or LA (most evidence, TGA-approved)
  • Second-line: Lisdexamfetamine (TGA-approved ≥6 years); dexamphetamine
  • Non-stimulants: Atomoxetine (4–12 weeks onset); Guanfacine XR (PBS-listed 6–17 years)
  • Weight-based dosing: 0.3–1 mg/kg/day methylphenidate (start 0.3, max 1 mg/kg or 60 mg)
  • Monitor height and weight every 3–6 months; plot on growth chart
  • Consider medication holidays (school holidays) if growth suppression is significant
  • Parent Management Training (PMT) is first-line for preschool children and important for all ages
  • School-based accommodations: extended time, preferential seating, movement breaks
Preschool children (<6 years) Non-pharmacological treatment (parent management training, behavioural interventions) is the recommended first-line approach. Methylphenidate may be considered in preschool children only when behavioural interventions have been optimised and ADHD is severely impairing — specialist decision only.
7Non-Pharmacological Management
Evidence-Based Non-Pharmacological Interventions
Psychoeducation (Essential — All Patients)
  • Neurobiological model of ADHD — reduces shame
  • Executive function deficits — working memory, inhibition, time blindness
  • ADHD as a lifelong trait, not a character flaw
  • Impact on relationships, work, self-esteem
  • Medication as a tool, not a cure
  • Resources: ADHD Australia, CHADD (international), AADPA guidelines
CBT for ADHD
  • Structured, skills-based (not insight-based like traditional CBT)
  • Targets: time management, organisation, procrastination, emotional regulation
  • Can be delivered via telehealth effectively
  • Programs: "Mastering Your Adult ADHD" (Safren); "ADHD in Adults" (Bramham)
ADHD Coaching
  • Goal-oriented; practical skills; real-world accountability
  • Telephone / video coaching highly effective
  • Complements medication well
Lifestyle — Strong Evidence
  • Exercise: 20–30 min aerobic daily → equivalent to low-dose stimulant acutely. Improves dopamine, executive function, mood.
  • Sleep: Critical — poor sleep dramatically worsens all ADHD symptoms. Prioritise sleep hygiene before adjusting medication dose.
  • Diet: Regular meals; avoid blood glucose fluctuations; omega-3 supplementation (modest evidence)
  • Mindfulness: Moderate evidence for attention and emotion regulation
Environmental Strategies
  • External systems (calendars, reminders, apps) — don't rely on working memory
  • Reduce environmental distractors (noise-cancelling headphones, clear workspace)
  • Time-boxing and structured routines
  • Workplace / study accommodations (extended deadlines, quiet rooms)
  • Body doubling (working alongside others — in person or virtual)
Relationships & Family
  • ADHD-informed couple therapy if relationship strain is significant
  • Partner psychoeducation — essential for understanding and reducing blame
  • Parent management training (children with ADHD)
  • Family therapy where indicated
